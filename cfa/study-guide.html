How to study for Level 1 (the evidence-based way)

This site is built around what cognitive science actually shows works. Ten minutes here will save you dozens of hours of low-value studying.

1. What the research says

Dunlosky et al.'s landmark review of ten study techniques found only two with high utility:

  • Retrieval practice (practice testing). Trying to recall an answer from memory strengthens it far more than re-reading it. Students who quiz themselves retain dramatically more a week later than students who re-studied the same material.
  • Spaced repetition. Reviewing material at growing intervals (1 day → 3 days → 1 week → 2+ weeks) produces 2–3× the retention of massed cramming, because each review happens just as the memory starts to fade.

Re-reading, highlighting, and summarizing — the strategies most people default to — rated low utility. They feel productive because the material feels familiar, but familiarity is not recall.

Combined, these become spaced retrieval: quiz yourself, on a schedule. That is exactly what this site's quizzes and flashcard scheduler do for you. Two more boosters used throughout the topic pages: worked examples (studying a fully-solved problem before attempting your own is one of the fastest ways for novices to learn quantitative material) and interleaving (mixed-topic mock exams beat one-topic-at-a-time review for the real exam, which shuffles all 10 topics together).

2. The study loop for every section

  1. Read the section once, actively. Work each worked example with your calculator instead of just reading the solution.
  2. Quiz immediately. Do the section's questions in the topic quiz. Commit to an answer before revealing — guessing then correcting is where learning happens.
  3. Mark the section complete so the dashboard tracks you.
  4. Review flashcards daily. 10–15 minutes on the flashcards page clears whatever the scheduler says is due. This is the single highest-value habit.
  5. Mock exams in the final 4–6 weeks. Take timed, weighted mocks on the exam page; drill whatever topics come back red.

Ethics tip: candidates near the passing borderline are traditionally helped by strong Ethics performance (CFA Institute applies extra scrutiny to Ethics at the margin). At 15–20% it is the single heaviest topic — never leave it to the last week alone; do its quiz repeatedly.

4. Exam-day tactics

  • 90 seconds per question. If you're stuck at 2 minutes, mark your best guess and move on — every question is worth the same.
  • Answer everything. No negative marking; a blind guess is worth ⅓ of a point in expectation.
  • Read the last line of the question first on long vignettes — know what's being asked before you read the setup.
  • "Closest to" means estimate. Many quantitative answers can be reached by approximation or by eliminating the two distractors built from classic mistakes (wrong sign, wrong compounding, wrong tail).
