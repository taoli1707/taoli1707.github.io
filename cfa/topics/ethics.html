Ethical & Professional Standards Exam weight: 15–20%

Ethics is the single heaviest topic on the Level 1 exam, and CFA Institute uses it as a tiebreaker for borderline scores. Nearly every question is a mini-scenario: a named analyst does something, and you must decide whether a specific Standard was violated. Master the seven Standards and the handful of fine distinctions the exam loves to test, and this topic becomes your most reliable source of points.

On this page

    1. Ethics and Trust in the Investment Profession

    This module sets up the vocabulary the rest of the topic uses: what ethics is, how it differs from law, why people behave unethically even when they know better, and how a structured decision framework helps. The exam tests these ideas directly with definition and "most likely" questions.

    What ethics is

    Ethics is the study of moral principles — beliefs about what is good, acceptable, and obligatory behavior. Ethical conduct is behavior that follows those principles and balances self-interest against both the direct consequences for others and the wider consequences for society. A profession is an occupational group that has a specialized body of knowledge, a service orientation (client interests first), and a shared code of ethics — a written set of principles adopted by the group. Professions self-regulate: they set entry standards, monitor conduct, and sanction members. The investment profession qualifies because clients must trust practitioners with their savings while being unable to fully evaluate the quality of the advice they receive.

    Ethics versus law

    The two overlap but are not the same, and the exam expects you to keep them apart:

    LawEthics
    SourceCreated by governments and regulators; varies by jurisdictionDerived from shared moral principles; can apply universally within a profession
    TimingReactive — laws are usually written after a problem appearsProactive — ethical principles anticipate situations laws have not yet addressed
    StandardMinimum acceptable behavior; "legal" is a floor, not a goalOften demands more than the law; some legal acts are unethical
    EnforcementCourts, regulators, fines, prisonProfessional sanctions, loss of reputation and trust

    Some acts are legal but unethical (trading on material nonpublic information in a country that does not prohibit it; recommending a legal but clearly unsuitable product for a bigger commission). Some acts are ethical but illegal (whistleblowing where local law forbids disclosure, done to protect clients or market integrity). The CFA Institute Standards frequently require conduct stricter than local law.

    Challenges to ethical behavior

    Knowing what is right does not guarantee doing it. The curriculum highlights three obstacles:

    • Overconfidence bias. Most people rate themselves more ethical than average, so they underestimate the chance they will face — and fail — an ethical test. Overconfident people rely on internal moral compasses and skip the deliberate analysis a situation deserves.
    • Situational influences. External pressures — money, prestige, loyalty to colleagues or a boss, fear of losing a job or a client — shape behavior far more than people expect. Large financial rewards and social pressure to conform are the classic examples. Research shows situational influences typically dominate personal character traits in predicting behavior: good people in bad environments do bad things.
    • Focus on the immediate. Situational influences make people concentrate on short-term, personal, concrete rewards (the bonus this quarter) and neglect long-term, diffuse costs (reputation, client trust, market integrity).

    Key point. On the exam, the "most likely" reason a generally honest person commits a violation is a situational influence (bonus pressure, loyalty to a supervisor), not a defective moral character. And a strong compliance culture matters more than hiring "good people."

    Ethical decision-making frameworks

    Because intuition fails under pressure, the curriculum recommends a structured framework. The general model has four phases (often summarized as Identify – Consider – Decide/Act – Reflect):

    • Identify: the relevant facts, the stakeholders and duties owed to each, applicable ethical principles, and any conflicts of interest.
    • Consider: situational influences acting on you, additional guidance (compliance, mentors, counsel), and alternative actions. Seeking an outside perspective is explicitly recommended — it counteracts your own biases.
    • Decide and act: make the decision and carry it out.
    • Reflect: afterward, assess the outcome. Was it what you intended? What would you do differently? Reflection converts experience into better future judgment.

    Why trust matters in finance

    Investment products are intangible — a client hands over cash today for a promise about the future and cannot inspect quality the way a car buyer can. There is also a large information asymmetry: the professional knows far more than the client. Trust is therefore the industry's foundation. When trust is high, savers commit capital, markets deepen, the cost of capital falls, and capital flows to its most productive uses — benefiting society broadly. When scandals destroy trust, investors withdraw, liquidity dries up, and everyone's cost of doing business rises. Ethical behavior is not just morally required; it is what makes the whole system work.

    Mini-scenario. Rojas, a junior analyst, is told by her supervisor to "shade" a valuation upward so the firm's investment-banking arm can win a mandate. Rojas privately believes she is highly ethical and has never faced such pressure before, so she trusts her instinct, complies, and rationalizes that "everyone adjusts models." Which challenge(s) to ethical behavior does this illustrate?

    Solution. Two at once. Overconfidence bias: Rojas assumed her personal ethics would carry her through without deliberate analysis, so she never worked through a decision framework. Situational influence: loyalty and pressure from a supervisor (and implicitly, career/compensation concerns) pushed her toward the short-term concrete reward and away from long-term considerations like client trust. This is also a conduct violation (misrepresentation), but the question here is about why a normally honest person slipped — situational influence is the exam's favorite answer.

    Exam trap. "Legal" is not a synonym for "ethical." A choice that says conduct is acceptable "because it is legal in that country" is almost always wrong — the Standards require members to follow the stricter of applicable law and the Code and Standards.

    2. Code of Ethics and Standards of Professional Conduct

    This module describes the machinery: the Professional Conduct Program that enforces the rules, the six components of the Code of Ethics, and the seven Standards of Professional Conduct. Expect direct questions on who is covered, how enforcement works, and what the components/Standards say.

    Who is covered and how enforcement works

    All CFA Institute members and candidates (anyone enrolled to sit for a CFA exam) must comply with the Code and Standards, regardless of job title, local law, or whether their employer has its own code. The Professional Conduct Program (PCP), overseen by the CFA Institute Board of Governors through the Disciplinary Review Committee (DRC), enforces them.

    • How inquiries start: self-disclosure on the annual Professional Conduct Statement (members must disclose complaints, litigation, or investigations); written complaints from others; evidence in media or other public sources; a report by exam proctors; and analysis of exam materials and scores (e.g., cheating patterns, leaked questions on social media).
    • Process: Professional Conduct staff investigate — requesting a written explanation, interviewing the member, complainants, and third parties, and collecting documents. Staff may conclude no violation occurred, issue a cautionary letter, or propose a sanction. A member who rejects a proposed sanction may request a hearing before a DRC panel.
    • Sanctions: public censure, suspension of membership and use of the CFA designation, revocation of the charter, or (for candidates) suspension or prohibition from further participation in the CFA Program. CFA Institute cannot fine or imprison anyone — its sanctions are professional, not legal.

    The six components of the Code of Ethics

    Members and candidates must:

    • Act with integrity, competence, diligence, and respect, and in an ethical manner with the public, clients, prospective clients, employers, employees, colleagues, and other market participants.
    • Place the integrity of the investment profession and the interests of clients above their own personal interests.
    • Use reasonable care and exercise independent professional judgment when analyzing investments, making recommendations, taking investment actions, and engaging in other professional activities.
    • Practice and encourage others to practice in a professional and ethical manner that reflects credit on themselves and the profession.
    • Promote the integrity and viability of global capital markets for the ultimate benefit of society.
    • Maintain and improve their professional competence and strive to maintain and improve the competence of other investment professionals.

    The seven Standards of Professional Conduct

    StandardNameOne-line summary

    IIIntegrity of Capital MarketsDon't act on material nonpublic information; don't manipulate markets.

    IVDuties to EmployersLoyalty to employer; written consent for outside compensation; supervisors must supervise.

    A useful mnemonic for the order: "Professionals In Cars Drive Extremely Into Conflicts, Responsibly" — or simply memorize P-I-C-M / Clients-Employers-Analysis-Conflicts-Institute. Note the priority logic embedded in the ordering: market integrity and client interests come before employer interests, which come before your own.

    Mini-scenario. Chen, a Level 2 candidate, receives a Professional Conduct inquiry after a proctor reports he kept writing after time was called. Chen ignores the staff's request for a written explanation, reasoning that "candidates aren't members, so the PCP has no jurisdiction over me." Is he right?

    Solution. No. The Code and Standards — and PCP jurisdiction — apply to all members and candidates in the CFA Program. Proctor reports are an explicit source of inquiries. Refusing to cooperate with a PCP investigation is itself a violation. Possible outcomes for Chen include suspension or prohibition from continued participation in the CFA Program.

    Key point. Sanctions are imposed by CFA Institute (via the DRC), and the worst outcomes are revocation of the charter or expulsion from the program — never fines or jail. A member who disagrees with a proposed sanction gets a hearing panel, not a court.

    Exam trap. Don't confuse the six components of the Code (broad aspirational principles) with the seven Standards (specific conduct rules). A question may quote a Code component and ask you to identify it as Code, not Standard.

    3. Standard I — Professionalism

    Standard I has four parts: Knowledge of the Law, Independence and Objectivity, Misrepresentation, and Misconduct. It is among the most heavily tested Standards because each part generates crisp violation/no-violation scenarios.

    I(A) Knowledge of the Law

    Members must understand and comply with all applicable laws, rules, and regulations (including the Code and Standards) governing their professional activities. Two operational rules dominate the exam:

    • The stricter-law rule. When applicable law and the Code and Standards conflict, comply with the more strict of the two. If local law is stricter than the Code, follow local law. If the Code is stricter than (or local law is silent on) an issue, follow the Code. Where no local law exists at all, the Code and Standards govern.
    • Dissociation. Members must not knowingly participate or assist in violations, and must dissociate from any ongoing illegal or unethical activity. Recommended escalation: first bring the matter to a supervisor or compliance; if the conduct continues, dissociate — which may require refusing to work on the account, asking for a different assignment, or ultimately resigning. Merely staying silent while continuing to work on the tainted business is not dissociation. Inaction combined with continued association may be construed as knowing participation.

    Important nuances: members are not required to report violations to governmental or regulatory authorities (though it may be prudent or required by law), and there is no requirement to report violations by others to CFA Institute (though it is encouraged). Members are not expected to be legal experts — but they must seek advice from compliance or counsel when in doubt.

    I(B) Independence and Objectivity

    Members must use reasonable care and judgment to maintain independence and objectivity, and must not offer, solicit, or accept any gift, benefit, compensation, or consideration that could reasonably be expected to compromise their independence and objectivity. The most-tested applications:

    • Gifts from issuers or would-be influencers: lavish gifts or entertainment from a company an analyst covers, or from a business seeking to influence a decision, must be refused. Modest gifts and entertainment (token value) are acceptable.
    • Gifts from clients: treated more leniously because a client's gift for past performance is not seen as compromising future objectivity toward third parties — but it may create a conflict with other clients (a favored-client incentive). Best practice per the Standard: disclose to the employer; gifts contingent on future performance require employer consent before acceptance (this also intersects Standard IV(B)).
    • Issuer-paid research: permitted only if the analyst discloses the arrangement, and compensation is a flat fee agreed in advance, not contingent on the report's conclusions or recommendations. Accepting stock, options, or a fee tied to a favorable rating violates I(B).
    • Travel: when visiting a company or attending a conference, analysts should pay their own commercial travel and lodging when feasible. Accepting the issuer's corporate jet is a red flag; chartered/company transport is acceptable only when commercial travel is unavailable (e.g., a remote mine site) or in group due-diligence settings where independence is protected.
    • Sell-side pressure: analysts must not succumb to pressure from investment-banking colleagues, issuers threatening to cut off access, or institutional clients with large positions in a covered stock. Firms should use firewalls between research and banking and restrict analyst participation in roadshows.

    I(C) Misrepresentation

    Members must not knowingly make any misrepresentation — an untrue statement, an omission that makes a statement misleading — relating to investment analysis, recommendations, actions, or other professional activities. Big applications:

    • Guarantees. Never state or imply that a return is guaranteed on an investment whose returns are inherently uncertain. Stating facts is fine: describing an insured bank CD as guaranteed, or accurately explaining that a government bond's coupon is backed by the sovereign, is not a violation.
    • Plagiarism. Using others' work without attribution violates I(C) — copying research, quoting "leading analysts" without naming them, or presenting a colleague's model as your own after changing a few inputs. Two carve-outs: you may use recognized financial and statistical reporting services (e.g., exchange data, Bloomberg quotes) without attribution, and work you produced for your employer can be used by the firm even after the author leaves (the firm owns it). But if you leave and reuse your old firm's models as your own, that is plagiarism (and a IV(A) problem).
    • Credentials and performance. Don't inflate qualifications, claim services you can't deliver, or overstate performance ("we beat the index every year"). Composite or benchmark cherry-picking belongs under III(D) but often co-occurs.
    • Omissions. Leaving out a known model limitation or the fact that returns are simulated rather than actual is a misrepresentation by omission.

    I(D) Misconduct

    Members must not engage in any professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit, or commit any act that reflects adversely on their professional reputation, integrity, or competence. Two boundaries matter:

    • The Standard targets professional conduct and acts bearing on professional reputation — not personal life generally. Personal bankruptcy from bad luck is not a violation; bankruptcy involving fraudulent conduct is. A civil-disobedience arrest at a protest is generally not misconduct; repeated intoxication at lunch that impairs professional judgment, or lying on an expense report, is.
    • Since 2022, the Standard also covers competence-related failures and, explicitly, conduct such as harassment and discrimination in professional contexts.

    Mini-scenario 1 (I(A)). Okafor, a CFA charterholder, works in a country with no insider-trading law. Her firm's headquarters is in a country whose law is also silent, but she learns material nonpublic information and wants to trade. Violation if she trades?

    Solution. Yes. Where applicable law is less strict than the Code and Standards — or absent — members must follow the Code and Standards. Standard II(A) prohibits trading on material nonpublic information regardless of local legality, so trading violates both I(A) (failing to follow the stricter standard) and II(A).

    Mini-scenario 2 (I(A) dissociation). Silva discovers her team is marketing a fund using inflated performance numbers. She emails compliance, who take no action, and she continues preparing the marketing decks "because it's my job." Violation?

    Solution. Yes. Reporting to compliance was the right first step, but when the violation continued she was required to dissociate — stop working on the misleading materials, escalate further, and if necessary resign. Continuing to prepare the decks is knowing participation in the violation. Note: she is not required to report the firm to the regulator under the Standards (local law may separately require it).

    Mini-scenario 3 (I(B)). A cement company Novak covers offers to fly him on its corporate jet to a remote plant with no commercial service, provide standard lodging, and give him a branded coffee mug. Separately, it offers a weekend at a luxury resort with spouses invited. What may he accept?

    Solution. The jet to the inaccessible site (commercial alternatives unavailable — an accepted exception), ordinary-course lodging, and the token mug are acceptable. The luxury resort weekend is lavish entertainment reasonably expected to compromise objectivity — he must decline it. Best practice is for his firm to reimburse whatever costs it can.

    Mini-scenario 4 (I(C)). Dupont tells prospects: "Based on our model, this structured note should return about 8%; historically similar notes have never lost money, so your principal is effectively guaranteed." The note is not principal-protected. Violation?

    Solution. Yes — I(C) Misrepresentation. Implying a guarantee on an investment with uncertain returns is prohibited, even hedged with "effectively." Giving the model-based 8% expectation clearly labeled as an estimate would have been fine; the guarantee language is the violation.

    Mini-scenario 5 (I(D)). Two facts about Ivanova: (1) she was arrested at a peaceful environmental protest; (2) she padded three taxi receipts on her expense report by small amounts. Which violates I(D)?

    Solution. Only (2). Civil disobedience unrelated to professional integrity is not misconduct. Padding expense reports — however small — is dishonesty and deceit that reflects adversely on professional integrity, so it violates I(D).

    Key point. I(A): follow the strictest applicable rule; dissociate from ongoing violations (reporting to regulators is optional under the Standards). I(B): refuse gifts from issuers unless token; disclose client gifts; issuer-paid research only for a flat, non-contingent fee.

    Exam trap. "She reported it to compliance, so she's done" — wrong if the misconduct continues. And "the gift was from a grateful client, so she must refuse it" — also wrong; client gifts for past performance require disclosure, not refusal.

    4. Standard II — Integrity of Capital Markets

    Standard II protects the market itself rather than a particular client. Its two parts — Material Nonpublic Information and Market Manipulation — supply some of the exam's trickiest scenarios, especially around the mosaic theory.

    II(A) Material Nonpublic Information

    Members who possess material nonpublic information (MNPI) that could affect the value of an investment must not act or cause others to act on it. Unpack the definitions:

    • Material: disclosure would likely affect the security's price, or a reasonable investor would want it before deciding. Earnings surprises, merger talks, major discoveries, dividend changes, ratings changes, and imminent large orders are classic examples. Ambiguous, unreliable, or vague information is less likely to be material — materiality depends partly on the source's reliability.
    • Nonpublic: not yet disseminated to the marketplace generally. Telling a room of analysts is selective disclosure, not public release; information is public once investors at large can access it (press release, filing, webcast open to all). Note: analysts who receive selective disclosure from a company cannot trade or share it until it is public.
    • Act or cause others to act: you cannot trade, tip friends or family, or change recommendations based on MNPI — even to avoid a loss, and even for accounts (like family accounts) where you have no financial interest.

    Mosaic theory — the crucial carve-out. An analyst may combine public information with nonmaterial nonpublic information (e.g., a plant manager's tone, industry chatter, channel checks) and reach a conclusion that would be material if a company had disclosed it directly. Acting on such a mosaic-based conclusion is not a violation — this is exactly what good analysts do. Keep records of the research to demonstrate the mosaic. The violation line: if any single input is itself material and nonpublic, the mosaic defense fails.

    Firm-level protections: information barriers/firewalls between investment banking and research/trading, restricted lists (better than watch lists made public, which signal the deal), physical and electronic separation, compliance review of interdepartmental communication, and heightened trade surveillance. When a firm comes into possession of MNPI (e.g., advising on a merger), the recommended action is to place the security on a restricted list and issue only factual, ordinary-course commentary. A recommended firm-wide practice is to encourage issuers to make information public promptly.

    II(B) Market Manipulation

    Members must not engage in practices that distort prices or artificially inflate trading volume with the intent to mislead market participants. Two families:

    • Transaction-based manipulation: trades made to mislead — wash trades (simultaneous buy/sell to fake volume), painting the tape, pump-and-dump, securing a dominant position in an asset to manipulate the price of it or a related derivative (a corner or squeeze).
    • Information-based manipulation: spreading false or misleading information — rumors on social media to move a price, exaggerated "research" designed to pump a position you plan to sell.

    Intent is the key. Legitimate trading strategies that happen to move prices, or exploiting a genuinely discovered market inefficiency, are not manipulation. Even a liquidity-provision arrangement (e.g., paying market makers to trade a new futures contract to attract volume) can be acceptable if the arrangement is disclosed; concealing it to make liquidity look organic is manipulation.

    Mini-scenario 1 (mosaic — no violation). Haddad covers a retailer. She combines public filings, satellite parking-lot images she bought commercially, and conversations with mall managers (each individually trivial) and concludes holiday sales will badly miss guidance. She downgrades the stock and her clients sell before the miss is announced. Violation?

    Solution. No violation — this is the mosaic theory. Every input was either public or nonmaterial nonpublic; the material conclusion was her own synthesis. She should retain her notes and data to document the mosaic if questioned. Had a company insider instead told her "we will miss guidance," that single material nonpublic fact would poison the conclusion and trading would violate II(A).

    Mini-scenario 2 (II(A) — violation without personal gain). Working late, Traoré overhears two investment bankers in the elevator discussing a client's imminent tender offer for Target Co. He doesn't trade himself, but he tells his sister to sell her Target Co puts, and he moves his discretionary client accounts out of a competitor likely to be hurt. Violations?

    Solution. Yes, on both counts. Causing others to act (tipping his sister) violates II(A) even though he earned nothing. Trading client accounts on the information also violates II(A) — the prohibition covers acting for any account, and trading in economically related securities on MNPI is still acting on MNPI. That the information was overheard rather than sought does not matter: he possesses MNPI from a reliable source (the bankers on the deal).

    Mini-scenario 3 (II(B)). To boost quarter-end reported volume in a thinly traded fund, Meyer executes offsetting buy and sell orders between two accounts he controls. Separately, Osei posts an anonymous but honestly held negative analysis of a stock she is short. Which violates II(B)?

    Solution. Meyer violates II(B): offsetting trades that create artificial volume with intent to mislead are transaction-based manipulation (wash trading). Osei does not violate II(B) merely by publishing genuinely held, well-founded analysis while short — there is no intent to deceive (though she should disclose her position under VI(A), and fabricating claims would flip it into information-based manipulation).

    Key point. Mosaic = public + nonmaterial nonpublic → conclusions are yours to use. MNPI = cannot act or cause action, for anyone's benefit, even to avoid losses. Manipulation requires intent to mislead; disclosed liquidity arrangements and honest analysis are not manipulation.

    Exam trap. "The analyst pieced it together herself, so she can't trade until the company announces" — wrong; that reverses the mosaic theory. Also watch for the distractor that says selective disclosure to a group of analysts makes information public. It doesn't.

    5. Standard III — Duties to Clients

    Standard III is the heart of the client relationship: loyalty and care, fair dealing, suitability, honest performance reporting, and confidentiality. It generates more exam scenarios than any other Standard, so learn each sub-part's trigger facts.

    III(A) Loyalty, Prudence, and Care

    Members owe clients loyalty, must act with reasonable care and prudent judgment, must act for the client's benefit, and must place client interests before their employer's and their own. Key applications:

    • Identify the client. For a fund or index manager, the "client" is the investors in the fund or the mandate/IPS itself, not the fund sponsor. For pension plans, the duty runs to plan beneficiaries, not the sponsoring company's management.
    • Soft dollars (client brokerage). Brokerage commissions are the client's asset. Directing trades to a broker who charges more or executes worse in exchange for perks to the manager violates III(A). Soft-dollar arrangements are acceptable only if the client receives best execution and the goods/services purchased with commissions directly benefit the client (e.g., research used to manage that client's account — not office rent or marketing). Under client-directed brokerage, a client may direct commissions for the client's own benefit; the manager should disclose that the client may not be getting best execution and seek best execution regardless.
    • Proxy voting. Proxies have economic value. Managers should vote proxies in clients' best interest under a disclosed policy. A cost–benefit analysis may justify not voting every routine proxy, but a blanket policy of never voting, or voting mechanically with management, is inconsistent with loyalty and care.

    III(B) Fair Dealing

    Members must deal fairly and objectively with all clients when providing analysis, making recommendations, or taking investment action. "Fairly" does not mean "equally" — firms may sell service tiers (premium clients get calls, others get email) as long as tiers are disclosed and available to all who pay, and no group is disadvantaged on the substance.

    • Dissemination of recommendations: material changes in recommendations should reach all relevant clients as simultaneously as practical. Do not tip favored clients first; do not trade for discretionary accounts before clients have had a reasonable chance to act. Clients who traded contrary to a new recommendation before learning of it should be advised of the change before the firm accepts their order.
    • IPO and oversubscribed-issue allocation: allocate on a fair basis — typically pro rata to all suitable clients (based on order size or a disclosed policy). Do not favor personal, family, or high-fee accounts. Members should forgo their own allocations of oversubscribed ("hot") issues so client demand is met, and family accounts that are ordinary fee-paying clients must be treated like any other client — neither favored nor excluded.

    III(C) Suitability

    In an advisory relationship, members must: (1) make reasonable inquiry into the client's investment experience, risk and return objectives, and financial constraints before acting, and reassess/update regularly (annually is the common benchmark, and after major life events); (2) determine an investment is suitable to the client's situation and mandate before recommending it; and (3) judge suitability in the context of the client's total portfolio, not in isolation — a risky security can be suitable inside a diversified portfolio, and a "safe" concentrated position can be unsuitable.

    • The investment policy statement (IPS) — objectives (risk, return) and constraints (liquidity, time horizon, taxes, legal/regulatory, unique needs) — is the tool. Write it at the start of the relationship; update it regularly.
    • Fund/index managers: suitability means staying true to the stated mandate and style; investors chose the fund based on it. Deviating from the stated strategy (style drift) violates III(C).
    • Unsolicited trade requests: if a client insists on an unsuitable trade, the member should discuss why it is unsuitable. If it is a minor position, documenting the discussion and executing may be acceptable; if it would materially change the portfolio, the member may need the client to update the IPS or should consider whether to execute at all (following firm policy, possibly executing only with a written acknowledgment or declining).
    • If a member manages to a mandate without an advisory relationship (e.g., executes only), the full suitability duty does not apply the same way — but fair dealing and loyalty still do.

    III(D) Performance Presentation

    When communicating performance information, members must make reasonable efforts to ensure it is fair, accurate, and complete. Applications: don't state or imply that past results will be repeated; don't cherry-pick your best accounts or best periods; include terminated portfolios in historical composites (dropping losers inflates results — survivorship bias); disclose whether results are gross or net of fees, and whether simulated/back-tested rather than actual; if you present performance you achieved at a prior firm, label it clearly as such and only if you can substantiate it. Brief presentations are fine if they disclose their limits and offer full detail on request. Complying with GIPS (Section 10) is the recommended, but not required, way to meet III(D).

    III(E) Preservation of Confidentiality

    Members must keep information about current, former, and prospective clients confidential unless: (1) the information concerns illegal activities by the client (disclosure duties then depend on law — members may be required to report, and should consult counsel/compliance); (2) disclosure is required by law (subpoena, regulator); or (3) the client permits it. Cooperating with a CFA Institute PCP investigation is also permitted — providing client information to the PCP is considered an extension of your obligations and not a confidentiality breach (unless local law forbids it). Practical care: don't discuss clients in elevators or on social media; protect electronic records; sharing client info with a colleague working on the same client's behalf is fine, but idle disclosure to an uninvolved colleague or a spouse is not.

    Mini-scenario 1 (III(A) soft dollars). Fontaine routes all client trades to BroCo, which charges 2 cents/share above the best available rate but gives Fontaine's firm free portfolio-analytics software used for those same clients, plus tickets to a golf tournament. Violation?

    Solution. Partly. Client commissions may buy research/services that directly benefit the clients — the analytics software could qualify — but only if clients still receive best execution, which paying 2 cents/share extra calls into question, and the golf tickets benefit Fontaine, not clients, so paying for them with client commissions violates III(A). He must seek best execution and use commissions solely for client benefit.

    Mini-scenario 2 (III(B) fair dealing). Petrova's firm upgrades MegaCorp to Buy at 9:00 a.m. She immediately calls her three largest clients, buys the stock for all discretionary accounts at 9:15, and the firm emails the full client list at 10:30. Violation?

    Solution. Yes — III(B). Material recommendation changes must be disseminated to all relevant clients as simultaneously as practical before favored clients or discretionary accounts act. Calling the big three first and trading discretionary accounts before the general release disadvantages the rest. A compliant approach: release to all clients at once (email/portal), then trade, treating all suitable accounts alike.

    Mini-scenario 3 (III(C) suitability). A 78-year-old client whose IPS states income and capital preservation asks Nakamura to put 5% of her portfolio into a speculative biotech she read about. Nakamura explains it doesn't fit the IPS; the client insists. Nakamura documents the conversation and executes the 5% position. Violation?

    Solution. Most likely no violation. For an unsolicited trade that is a small part of the total portfolio, discussing unsuitability, documenting, and executing is acceptable — suitability is judged in a total-portfolio context, and 5% does not change the portfolio's character. If she had wanted 50%, Nakamura should require an updated IPS reflecting the changed risk profile or decline/segregate the trade per firm policy.

    Mini-scenario 4 (III(D)/III(E)). Marketing deck: "Our small-cap composite returned 14% annually over 10 years" — the composite excludes three accounts that closed after poor results, and returns are simulated for the first four years without saying so. Also, over dinner, the manager tells a friend that a famous client is liquidating his account. Violations?

    Solution. Two. III(D): excluding terminated accounts (survivorship bias) and failing to label simulated results makes the presentation neither fair, accurate, nor complete. III(E): revealing a client's dealings to an uninvolved friend breaches confidentiality — none of the three exceptions (illegal activity, required by law, client permission) applies.

    Key point. Client commissions belong to the client (best execution + client benefit). Fair dealing means simultaneous dissemination and pro-rata hot-issue allocation with family client accounts treated like any other client. Suitability is portfolio-context and requires an IPS reviewed at least annually. Confidentiality survives the relationship but yields to law, illegality, client consent — and the PCP.

    Exam trap. Fair dealing ≠ equal dealing: premium service tiers are fine if disclosed and open to all. And a family account that pays regular fees must NOT be excluded from an IPO allocation "to be safe" — exclusion is itself unfair dealing.

    6. Standard IV — Duties to Employers

    Standard IV governs the member–employer relationship: loyalty while employed, permission for outside pay, and supervisors' duties. The leaving-an-employer fact patterns are exam staples.

    IV(A) Loyalty

    In matters related to their employment, members must act for the benefit of their employer and not deprive the employer of their skills, divulge confidential information, or otherwise cause harm. Client duties still rank above employer duties, but the employer outranks the member's personal interests. The tested applications:

    • While still employed: you may prepare to leave (arrange office space, incorporate, take licensing exams) on your own time, but you may not solicit the employer's clients or divert business opportunities before resigning, misappropriate materials, or recruit colleagues in ways that harm the employer while you still work there.
    • What you cannot take when you leave: client lists and contact details, models, spreadsheets, research reports, marketing materials — anything created on employer time/resources belongs to the employer, even work you personally created. Taking files (paper or electronic) without permission violates IV(A).
    • What you can do after leaving: use skills, experience, and knowledge carried in your head; contact former clients using public sources (directories, LinkedIn) provided you have no enforceable non-compete/non-solicit and the information isn't taken from employer records. Simple knowledge of who clients are, retained in memory, is generally usable — reconstructing the employer's confidential list is not.
    • Independent practice while employed: permitted only after notifying the employer of the nature, duration, and compensation of the services and obtaining consent before undertaking activity that could compete.
    • Whistleblowing: violating employer confidentiality can be justified only when done to protect clients or the integrity of capital markets — never for personal gain. Reporting the employer's illegal activity to regulators in good faith does not violate IV(A).
    • Nature of employment: independent contractors owe the duty of loyalty within the agreed scope; employees owe it broadly. Voluntary/unpaid work for an employer still creates the duty.

    IV(B) Additional Compensation Arrangements

    Members must not accept gifts, benefits, compensation, or consideration that competes with or might create a conflict with the employer's interest unless they obtain written consent from all parties involved — in practice, the employer and the outside payer. The classic trigger: a client offers a bonus (cash, vacation, use of a condo) contingent on the future performance of their account. The member must disclose terms in writing and get the employer's written consent before accepting. "Written" includes email. Contrast with I(B): a modest thank-you gift for past service needs disclosure but not prior written consent; performance-linked future arrangements need prior written consent under IV(B).

    IV(C) Responsibilities of Supervisors

    Members with supervisory authority must make reasonable efforts to ensure that anyone subject to their supervision complies with applicable laws, rules, regulations, and the Code and Standards — and to detect and prevent violations.

    • A supervisor should not accept supervisory responsibility if the firm has an inadequate compliance system — instead, decline in writing until the firm adopts reasonable procedures.
    • Adequate procedures: a clear, distributed compliance manual; training; monitoring; checks on outside accounts; escalation paths. Delegation of tasks is allowed, but it does not relieve the supervisor of responsibility.
    • When a violation is discovered: respond promptly, conduct a thorough investigation, and place appropriate limits on the wrongdoer pending the outcome (restrict activities, increase monitoring). Merely warning the employee and telling them "don't do it again," or relying on the employee's assurance, is not enough.
    • A supervisor with no subordinate misconduct can still violate IV(C) by failing to establish/implement reasonable procedures. Conversely, a supervisor who made genuinely reasonable efforts does not violate IV(C) just because a determined employee evaded the system.

    Mini-scenario 1 (IV(A) leaving). Before resigning, Adeyemi (a) rents office space and files incorporation papers for her new firm on weekends; (b) emails herself the valuation model she built at work "since I wrote it"; (c) after resigning, looks up former clients' phone numbers in a public directory and calls them (no non-solicit agreement). Which violate IV(A)?

    Solution. Only (b). Preparations to leave on personal time (a) are permitted. The model (b) belongs to the employer even though she authored it — taking it without permission violates IV(A) (and using it later as her own would add an I(C) plagiarism problem). Contacting former clients from public information after departure (c) is allowed absent a binding agreement, since she took no employer records.

    Mini-scenario 2 (IV(B)). A wealthy client tells Berg, "Beat 8% on my account this year and I'll lend you my ski chalet for two weeks." Berg thanks him, mentions it verbally to his supervisor who nods, and accepts. Violation?

    Solution. Yes — IV(B). The chalet is additional compensation contingent on future performance, which creates a conflict with the employer (an incentive to favor this account) and with other clients. Berg needed written consent from all parties — a verbal nod is insufficient. An email to the employer describing the terms, answered with written approval, would have cured it.

    Mini-scenario 3 (IV(C)). Compliance head Ricci learns a trader may be front-running client orders. Ricci interviews the trader, who denies it convincingly, so Ricci closes the matter with a stern warning and no restrictions. The trader front-runs again a month later. Did Ricci violate IV(C)?

    Solution. Yes. Once a potential violation surfaces, a supervisor must promptly initiate a real investigation and restrict the employee's activities (e.g., suspend trading authority, heighten monitoring) until it is resolved. Accepting the employee's assurance and issuing only a warning is inadequate supervision, so the second offense occurred under a deficient response for which Ricci is responsible.

    Key point. Skills and memory leave with you; records, models, and client lists stay. Future-performance perks from clients need prior written employer consent. Supervisors must build systems, and when misconduct surfaces must investigate and restrict — a warning alone never suffices.

    Exam trap. "She created the spreadsheet, so she may take it" — wrong; employer property. "The supervisor delegated compliance, so he's off the hook" — wrong; delegation never removes responsibility.

    7. Standard V — Investment Analysis, Recommendations, and Actions

    Standard V is about the quality of the work product: do enough research, communicate it honestly (separating fact from opinion), and keep the records that back it up.

    V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis

    Members must exercise diligence, independence, and thoroughness in analyzing investments, making recommendations, and taking actions, and must have a reasonable and adequate basis, supported by appropriate research, for any recommendation or action. Applications:

    • Level of diligence scales with the product's complexity and the role: quantitative models must be tested (including how they behave in negative markets and scenarios outside the data used to build them); relying on a rating or someone else's headline conclusion alone is not a reasonable basis.
    • Using secondary or third-party research is fine if the member makes reasonable efforts to verify it is sound — review the analysts' assumptions, rigor, and timeliness. The same applies to external advisers and fund selection: review their code of ethics, compliance, and adherence to stated strategy periodically.
    • Group research disagreement — the famous rule: if a member participates in group research and the group's consensus conclusion differs from the member's own view, the member does not need to dissociate from or withhold their name from the report, as long as the member believes the conclusion has a reasonable and adequate basis and reflects the team's independent, objective process. Dissociation is warranted only if the member doubts the process/basis itself. Documenting the difference of opinion is prudent.

    V(B) Communication with Clients and Prospective Clients

    Members must: (1) disclose the basic format and general principles of their investment processes and promptly disclose any material changes to those processes; (2) disclose significant limitations and risks associated with the investment process (leverage, liquidity, capacity, model limits); (3) use reasonable judgment to identify which factors are important and include them in communications; and (4) distinguish between fact and opinion in presenting analysis and recommendations.

    • "The company earned $2.10 last year" is fact. "Earnings will grow 12% annually for five years" is opinion/projection and must be labeled as such — presenting forecasts or model outputs as fact violates V(B).
    • A change such as adding derivatives overlays, switching from fundamental to quantitative selection, or changing the benchmark is material and must be communicated promptly to clients (before or as it happens — clients then decide whether the strategy still suits them).
    • Reports can be brief (a one-line rating change is fine) as long as detailed supporting information is maintained and available on request. "Communication" includes any medium — email, texts, social posts all count.

    V(C) Record Retention

    Members must develop and maintain appropriate records to support their analysis, recommendations, actions, and other investment-related communications with clients and prospects. Testable specifics:

    • Records are the property of the firm, not the member. When you leave, you may not take records (even ones you created) without the employer's consent — and you cannot rely on memory of old research at a new employer; you must recreate the supporting analysis from public or new sources.
    • Records may be hard copy or electronic; interview notes, model runs, data downloads, and even the social-media posts behind a recommendation should be retained.
    • Retention period: follow local regulation; if none applies, CFA Institute recommends at least seven years.
    • Maintaining records is usually the firm's responsibility operationally, but members must support it.

    Mini-scenario 1 (V(A) group research). Varga is on a five-analyst team covering airlines. The team concludes "Buy" on AeroCo; Varga believes "Hold" is right, but she reviewed the process and agrees the team's work is rigorous, independent, and its conclusion has a sound basis. She lets her name appear on the report. Violation?

    Solution. No violation. Under V(A), a dissenting member may remain on a group report if the conclusion has a reasonable and adequate basis from a sound, objective process — personal agreement with the conclusion is not required. She should document her differing view. She would need to request removal of her name only if she doubted the basis or integrity of the process itself.

    Mini-scenario 2 (V(B)). Kimura's fund quietly begins using 2× leverage and writes clients: "Our disciplined stock-selection process continues unchanged. We are confident the fund will deliver 15% next year, matching last year's actual result." Violations?

    Solution. Yes, two. Adding leverage is a material change to the investment process (and a significant new risk) that must be promptly disclosed — claiming the process is "unchanged" violates V(B)(1)/(2). And "will deliver 15%" states an opinion/forecast as fact, violating the fact-versus-opinion requirement (worded strongly enough, it also flirts with an I(C) implied guarantee). Last year's actual 15% may be stated as fact.

    Mini-scenario 3 (V(C)). Duval joins a new firm and, in week one, reissues his old "Buy" on ChemCo, telling clients "I've researched this name for years." He kept no files (his old firm retained them, correctly) and did no new work. Violation?

    Solution. Yes — V(C) and V(A). His prior research records belong to his former employer, and without records he lacks documented support at the new firm; memory is not a substitute. He must recreate a supporting analysis from public/new sources before reissuing the recommendation. Note the firm-ownership point cuts both ways: leaving the files behind was correct; reissuing without rebuilding the basis was not.

    Key point. Group report + personal disagreement + sound process = keep your name on it. Fact vs. opinion: anything about the future is opinion and must be labeled. Records belong to the firm; with no regulatory rule, keep them at least seven years; at a new firm, rebuild your basis before re-recommending.

    Exam trap. The distractor "she must dissociate from the group report because she disagrees" is wrong whenever the vignette says the process was sound. Also, "he may rely on his years of experience with the stock" at a new firm is wrong — no records, no basis.

    8. Standard VI — Conflicts of Interest

    Standard VI polices situations where the member's interests could collide with clients' or the employer's: disclose conflicts prominently, put client transactions first, and disclose referral arrangements.

    VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts (and Avoidance)

    Members must avoid or make full and fair disclosure of all matters that could reasonably be expected to impair their independence and objectivity or interfere with duties to clients, prospects, and employer. Disclosures must be prominent, in plain language, and communicate the relevant information effectively — burying a conflict in a footnote fails. What must be disclosed:

    • To clients/prospects: ownership of stock in companies you recommend, board seats or officer roles, investment-banking or market-making relationships between your firm and covered issuers, and compensation structures that create incentives (e.g., bonuses tied to short-term account performance, fees from fund sponsors).
    • To the employer: outside board service, personal stock ownership, and any relationship or benefit that could compromise your duties, so the employer can assess and manage it.
    • Updating: disclose conflicts when they arise and keep disclosures current. If a conflict cannot be effectively managed by disclosure, avoid it (e.g., sell the position or decline the coverage).

    VI(B) Priority of Transactions

    Investment transactions for clients and employers must have priority over transactions in which a member is a beneficial owner. "Beneficial owner" means the member has a direct or indirect personal interest — accounts of a spouse, minor children, and other accounts the member controls or benefits from.

    • Client and employer trades come first. Members may trade for themselves only after clients and employers have had an adequate opportunity to act on a recommendation. Front-running client orders is a core violation.
    • Family accounts that are ordinary clients: if a family member's account is a regular fee-paying client account, treat it like any other client account — it must not be disadvantaged relative to other clients, nor favored. Only accounts in which the member has beneficial ownership are subject to the personal-trading restrictions (pre-clearance, trading after clients).
    • Recommended firm procedures: restricted/watch lists, pre-clearance of employee trades, blackout windows around client trading, reporting of holdings and duplicate confirmations, restrictions on employee IPO participation and private placements.
    • Personal trading is not banned — it just goes last, and must not exploit knowledge of pending client orders or unreleased recommendations.

    VI(C) Referral Fees

    Members must disclose to their employer, clients, and prospective clients, as appropriate, any compensation, consideration, or benefit received from, or paid to, others for the recommendation of products or services. Timing matters: disclosure must occur before the client acts on the referral (before entering any formal agreement), so the client can evaluate the recommendation's true cost and any partiality. "Compensation" is read broadly — cash, discounted rent, reciprocal referrals ("I send you clients, you send me clients"), or a bonus pool credit all count. Interdepartmental referral arrangements inside one firm should also be disclosed to clients.

    Mini-scenario 1 (VI(A)). Anand initiates coverage of BankCo with a Buy. He owns 5,000 BankCo shares in his personal account and his firm co-managed BankCo's bond offering last quarter. The report's final page, in small print among boilerplate, says "employees may hold securities mentioned herein." Violation?

    Solution. Yes — VI(A). Both his personal ownership and the firm's investment-banking relationship are conflicts that must be disclosed prominently and specifically; generic small-print boilerplate does not "communicate the relevant information effectively." Proper practice: a clear, specific disclosure of his ownership and the banking relationship in the report, plus disclosure of his holding to his employer.

    Mini-scenario 2 (VI(B)). Ferreira's mother has a regular, full-fee discretionary account at his firm. The firm is allocating a hot IPO pro rata among suitable clients. Ferreira (a) excludes his mother's account "to avoid any appearance of impropriety," and (b) buys shares for his own account in the same allocation. Violations?

    Solution. Both are wrong, in opposite directions. (a) violates fair dealing/priority principles: a family account that is an ordinary fee-paying client must be treated like any other client — excluding it disadvantages a client. (b) violates VI(B): as a beneficial owner, Ferreira participates only after client demand is met, and members should generally forgo hot-issue allocations for themselves.

    Mini-scenario 3 (VI(C)). An accounting firm sends Weiss's advisory firm one referral per month; in exchange, Weiss's firm does the accountants' partners' tax-season financial plans free. Weiss lands a referred client, mentions nothing, signs the advisory agreement, and discloses the arrangement in the first quarterly letter. Violation?

    Solution. Yes — VI(C). The reciprocal benefit (free planning work) is referral compensation even though no cash changes hands, and disclosure must be made before the client enters the agreement, not after. The client was entitled to weigh the referral's partiality and full cost first. Disclosing the nature and value of the benefit at the first meeting would have cured it.

    Key point. Conflicts: disclose prominently and specifically, or avoid. Trades: clients and employer first, you last; beneficial-owner accounts follow personal-trading rules; family accounts that are ordinary clients are treated as clients. Referrals: disclose the nature and value before the client engages — non-cash and reciprocal benefits count.

    Exam trap. Excluding a family client account from an allocation "to be extra ethical" is a violation, not a virtue. And boilerplate "may hold positions" disclaimers never satisfy VI(A).

    9. Standard VII — Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or Candidate

    Standard VII protects the CFA Program itself: don't compromise the integrity of the exams or the Institute, and use the designation accurately and with dignity.

    VII(A) Conduct as Participants in CFA Institute Programs

    Members and candidates must not engage in any conduct that compromises the reputation or integrity of CFA Institute or the CFA designation, or the integrity, validity, or security of CFA Institute programs. Tested applications:

    • Exam confidentiality: never share actual exam questions, broad topical coverage ("there were three questions on swaps"), formulas tested or not tested, or your opinion of specific questions — in person, online, or in chat groups, even after the exam. Cheating, writing after time is called, bringing prohibited devices, and impersonation all violate VII(A).
    • What you MAY discuss: the curriculum's published topic list, your general study approach, how you felt about the exam's overall difficulty ("it was hard"), and anything CFA Institute has made public.
    • Volunteers: graders and item writers must keep materials confidential; using insider knowledge of the exam process for gain violates VII(A).
    • Expressing opinions is allowed: members may criticize CFA Institute policies or the program itself — VII(A) restricts confidential information and integrity-compromising conduct, not free expression.

    VII(B) Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program

    When referring to CFA Institute, membership, the designation, or candidacy, members and candidates must not misrepresent or exaggerate the meaning or implications of any of them. Mechanics the exam loves:

    • "CFA" is used as an adjective, never a noun. Correct: "Maria Diaz, CFA" or "Maria Diaz is a CFA charterholder." Wrong: "Maria is a CFA" or "three CFAs work here." No plural "CFAs" as a noun; the marks should not be more prominent than the person's name.
    • Candidates: may say "Level 2 candidate in the CFA Program" only if registered for the next exam or awaiting results. There is no partial designation — never "CFA Level 2," "CFA (expected 2027)," or "passed Level 3, charter pending" as a credential-style label. Stating facts is fine: "passed all three levels on the first attempt" or "passed Level 3 in 2025; awaiting charter" (as a factual statement) is acceptable — implying superior ability because of it ("...and therefore will outperform") is not.
    • Membership maintenance: to use the designation you must remain a member in good standing — sign the annual Professional Conduct Statement and pay dues. Lapse either one and you must stop using the marks (you may still say factually that you passed the exams in given years).
    • No claim that the charter implies superior performance capability. Saying it reflects rigorous training and an ethical commitment is fine.

    Mini-scenario 1 (VII(A)). The evening after his exam window, Osman posts in a candidate forum: "Rough paper. Heads-up: heavy on FRA, almost nothing on derivatives, and there was a brutal question mixing pension accounting with FX." Violation?

    Solution. Yes — VII(A). Revealing the topical weighting of a specific exam and describing a specific question compromises exam security and the integrity of the program, even without quoting a question verbatim and even after the exam has ended. Saying only "I found it rough" would have been acceptable.

    Mini-scenario 2 (VII(B)). Three business cards: (a) "Lena Krug, CFA Level 2"; (b) "Lena Krug — Level 3 candidate in the CFA Program" (she is registered for the next Level 3 window); (c) "Lena Krug, CFA" printed in gold letters twice the size of her name (she is a charterholder in good standing). Which violate VII(B)?

    Solution. (a) and (c). (a) creates a partial designation — there is no such credential as "CFA Level 2." (b) is the correct candidate formulation, permitted while registered for the next exam. (c) misuses the marks: "CFA" must not be displayed more prominently than the member's name (oversized, decorative use exaggerates the designation).

    Mini-scenario 3 (VII(B)). Boone passed all three levels years ago but stopped paying CFA Institute dues and no longer files a Professional Conduct Statement. His LinkedIn headline reads "CFA charterholder." His résumé also says, "Passed Levels 1–3 of the CFA exams, 2016–2018." Violations?

    Solution. The headline violates VII(B): using the designation requires active membership in good standing (dues + annual PCS). The résumé line is acceptable — it is a factual statement about exams passed, which he may always make. He should remove "charterholder" until he reinstates his membership.

    Key point. VII(A): nothing about actual exam content — ever — but opinions about CFA Institute are fine. VII(B): CFA is an adjective; candidates are "candidates in the CFA Program" only while registered; no partial designations; keep dues and the PCS current or drop the marks.

    Exam trap. "The exam is over, so discussing which topics appeared is harmless" — still a violation. "She said she passed all three levels on her first try" — factual and allowed; the violation appears only if she implies superior ability because of it.

    10. Introduction to the Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS)

    GIPS gets a short, high-yield reading at Level 1. You need the purpose, who may claim compliance, the composite idea, and the facts about verification — all directly testable.

    Why GIPS exists

    Without a common standard, performance reporting invites abuse: showing only the best portfolios (cherry-picking), dropping dead accounts (survivorship bias), and choosing flattering measurement periods. Prospective clients then cannot compare managers fairly across firms and countries. The Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS) are voluntary, ethical standards — based on the principles of fair representation and full disclosure — for calculating and presenting investment performance so results are comparable across firms globally. They benefit both prospective clients (comparability, some assurance of integrity) and firms (a level playing field, credibility in all markets, strengthened internal controls).

    Who can claim compliance

    • Compliance may be claimed only by firms (an investment firm, subsidiary, or division held out to clients as a distinct business entity), and — in the current standards — by asset owners reporting to their oversight bodies. Individuals cannot claim GIPS compliance, cannot be "GIPS certified," and software cannot be "GIPS compliant."
    • Compliance is voluntary — no law requires it — and it is all-or-nothing on a firm-wide basis: there is no partial compliance ("GIPS compliant except for...") and no claiming compliance for only some products or composites.
    • Only firms that actually manage assets may claim compliance (consultants and vendors may not, though they may serve compliant firms).

    Composites — the core mechanism

    A composite is an aggregation of one or more portfolios managed according to a similar investment mandate, objective, or strategy. The anti-cherry-picking rule: a composite must include all actual, fee-paying, discretionary portfolios that fit the strategy — including terminated portfolios for the periods they were live (killing survivorship bias). Crucially, the criteria for including portfolios in a composite must be determined before performance is known ("on an ex ante basis"), not after — otherwise firms would define composites around known winners. Firms present composite (not individual-account) performance to prospects.

    Verification

    • Verification is performed by an independent third party — a firm cannot verify itself.
    • It is voluntary (recommended, not required) for claiming GIPS compliance.
    • It applies to the entire firm, not to specific composites — a single verification report covers the firm as a whole.
    • What it tests: whether the firm has complied with all composite construction requirements on a firm-wide basis, and whether its policies and procedures are designed to calculate and present performance in compliance with GIPS. It does not attest that any particular composite's returns are accurate.

    Mini-scenario. An advertisement states: "Petra Lind, CFA, is GIPS-certified, and her flagship Growth composite is GIPS compliant and independently verified, guaranteeing its returns are accurate." Identify the errors.

    Solution. Three. (1) Individuals cannot be GIPS-certified or claim compliance — only firms (and asset owners) can. (2) Compliance cannot attach to a single composite; it is firm-wide, all-or-nothing. (3) Verification is firm-wide and addresses composite-construction compliance and the design of policies/procedures — it does not guarantee the accuracy of any composite's performance. (Bonus: "guaranteeing" language is a misrepresentation problem too.)

    Key point. GIPS = voluntary ethical standards for fair, comparable performance presentation. Firms (not people), firm-wide (not per-product). Composites include all fee-paying discretionary portfolios in the strategy, defined ex ante, terminated accounts included. Verification: third-party, voluntary, firm-wide, procedures-focused.

    Exam trap. Every wrong GIPS choice tends to flip one of four switches: individual↔firm, required↔voluntary, per-composite↔firm-wide, or accuracy-guarantee↔procedures-check. Verify which switch the choice flips before answering.

    11. Ethics Application — Attacking Exam Vignettes

    Ethics questions reward a method, not memory alone. This section gives you the exam algorithm and one table collecting the distinctions the exam recycles year after year.

    How to attack an ethics vignette

    • Read the question stem first. It usually names the Standard ("...most likely violated Standard III(B)?") or asks "violation or not." Knowing what's asked filters the vignette's noise.
    • Identify the actor and the client. Whose money, whose duty? Fund investors vs. sponsor; plan beneficiaries vs. plan sponsor; employer vs. self.
    • Flag trigger words. "Guaranteed" → I(C). "Overheard/insider/pre-announcement" → II(A). "Before other clients" → III(B) or VI(B). "Took files/client list" → IV(A). "Verbal approval" → IV(B) (needs written). "Forecast stated as fact" → V(B). "Small print / boilerplate" → VI(A). "CFA Level 2" on a card → VII(B).
    • Apply the strictest applicable rule (law vs. Code) and remember the hierarchy: market integrity and clients > employer > self.
    • Beware "half-right" behavior. The classic wrong answers describe someone who did the first step (told compliance, disclosed verbally, documented the chat) but skipped the required second step (dissociate, get written consent, disclose before engagement).
    • Recommended vs. required. Many choices are wrong because they upgrade a recommendation (report to regulators, comply with GIPS, keep records 7 years absent regulation) into a requirement — or downgrade a requirement into a suggestion.
    • When two Standards seem to apply, the credited answer is usually the most specific one (front-running a client order is VI(B), even though it also breaches loyalty).

    Mini-scenario (putting it together). A vignette: "Ito tells her supervisor that a colleague is inflating fund NAVs. The supervisor says he'll handle it. Ito continues to distribute the fund's fact sheets. Two clients later ask Ito why the fund's reported returns beat its holdings-based returns; she says she is 'confident in the numbers.'" Which Standards are implicated, and what should Ito have done?

    Solution. I(A) Knowledge of the Law: after reporting, the violation continued, so Ito had to dissociate — stop distributing the tainted fact sheets and escalate (compliance, senior management); continuing distribution is knowing participation. I(C)/III(D): the fact sheets misrepresent performance, and her "confident in the numbers" statement to clients compounds the misrepresentation. She is not required by the Standards to report the firm to regulators (though law might require it, and whistleblowing to protect clients would not violate IV(A)). The exam's credited pattern: report → escalate → dissociate; never keep selling the product.

    Key point. Almost every ethics question turns on one of the table's rows. Drill the table until each row's "allowed vs. violation" pair is automatic, and always ask: did the actor complete every required step, in the required form (written? prior? prominent? simultaneous?), at the required time?

    Exam trap. The test writers' favorite trick is the virtuous-sounding wrong answer: "to be safe, she refused the client's thank-you gift / excluded her mother's account / resigned immediately without escalating." Over-strictness that harms a client or skips required process can itself be the wrong choice. Match the Standard's actual requirement — no more, no less.
