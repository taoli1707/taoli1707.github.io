Corporate Issuers Exam weight: 6–9%

Corporate Issuers looks at companies from the inside: how they are organized and governed, how they manage short-term cash, how they choose long-term investments, and how they finance themselves with debt and equity. It is the bridge between Financial Statement Analysis (where the numbers come from) and Equity and Fixed Income (how investors value the claims the company issues).

On this page

    1. Organizational Forms, Corporate Issuer Features, and Ownership

    The exam tests whether you can match a business's legal form to its features: who owns it, who runs it, who is liable for its debts, and how it is taxed. Almost every distinction below flows from one idea — whether the business is legally separate from its owners.

    The three broad organizational forms

    • Sole trader (sole proprietorship). One person owns and operates the business. The business is not a separate legal entity: the owner has unlimited liability (personal assets can be seized to pay business debts), profits are taxed once as the owner's personal income, and the business's life and financing capacity are limited to the owner's own resources.
    • Partnership. Two or more owners. In a general partnership (GP), all partners share responsibility for running the business and have unlimited liability — including for each other's business actions. A limited partnership (LP) has at least one general partner (unlimited liability, runs the business) plus limited partners whose liability is capped at their investment and who do not manage the business. Profits pass through to partners' personal taxes.
    • Limited company (corporation). A separate legal entity that can own assets, borrow, sue, and be sued in its own name. Owners (shareholders) have limited liability: the most they can lose is what they paid for their shares. Ownership and management are separated — shareholders elect a board of directors, which hires managers. The company has an unbounded life and, because ownership is divided into transferable shares, it has the greatest capacity to raise capital.

    Public vs. private companies

    The defining difference is how shares trade, not size or fame. A public company has shares listed on an exchange, so ownership changes hands between investors in the secondary market without the company's involvement; it faces ongoing disclosure and listing requirements. A private company's shares do not trade on an exchange — transfers are negotiated privately, are far less liquid, and the company has lighter disclosure obligations. Private companies raise equity through private placements (often to accredited/qualified investors).

    • Going public: initial public offering (IPO), direct listing (no new capital raised, existing shares simply begin trading), or acquisition by a listed company — including by a SPAC (special purpose acquisition company, a listed cash shell formed to buy a private business).
    • Going private: an acquirer (often a private equity fund in a leveraged buyout) buys all public shares and delists the company.
    • The global trend in many developed markets: fewer listed companies, with firms staying private longer because private capital (venture capital, private equity, private debt) is abundant.

    Share classes and shareholder rights

    Common shareholders are the residual owners: they get whatever is left after all other claims, and they typically vote on directors and major transactions. Companies can issue multiple share classes with different voting rights or dividend rights. In a dual-class structure, founders often hold a class with superior votes (say, 10 votes per share) while the public holds one-vote shares, letting founders keep control with a minority of the economic interest.

    Key point. Limited liability is what makes broad public ownership possible: an investor can hold a tiny stake in hundreds of companies without risking personal ruin from any one of them. Maximum possible loss = amount invested.
    Exam trap. "Public" does not mean government-owned, and it does not require an IPO — direct listings and SPAC mergers also create public companies. Also, limited partners in an LP have limited liability, but the general partner does not.

    Question. Three friends start a consulting firm. Ana wants to manage the business daily; Ben and Chen only want to invest cash and cap their downside at their investment. Ben also insists the firm keep operating even if one of them dies. Which organizational form best fits, and who bears unlimited liability?

    Solution. A limited partnership fits the liability split — Ana as general partner (manages, unlimited liability), Ben and Chen as limited partners (no management role, liability limited to their investment). But an LP's continuity can be affected by changes in partners, and Ben wants perpetual life. A limited company (corporation) satisfies everything: all three get limited liability, Ana can be hired as manager, and the entity has unlimited life. Trade-off: potential double taxation and more formation/compliance cost. If they choose the LP instead, Ana alone bears unlimited liability.

    2. Investors and Other Stakeholders

    A corporation is a web of claims. This module asks: who has a stake in the company, what does each group want, and how do the risk–return profiles of debt and equity differ? These contrasts feed directly into governance (next section) and into Fixed Income and Equity valuation.

    Debt vs. equity claims

    Debtholders have a contractual claim: fixed interest and principal on set dates, enforceable in court, with priority over equity in liquidation. Their upside is capped — the best outcome is getting paid exactly what was promised — so their return profile is asymmetric: limited upside, large downside if the firm defaults. Debtholders therefore care mostly about downside risk and prefer stability and ample cash flow coverage.

    Equityholders have a residual claim: they are paid last, dividends are discretionary, and in liquidation they get whatever remains (often nothing). In exchange, their upside is unlimited — equity value rises without bound as the firm's value grows, while limited liability caps the loss at the investment. Equityholders benefit from risk-taking in a way debtholders do not.

    Key point. Equity is like a call option on the firm's assets (strike = face value of debt); debt is like owning the firm minus that call. That is why shareholders may prefer riskier projects — volatility raises option value — while lenders resist. From the issuer's side, debt is the cheaper financing (lower required return plus tax-deductible interest) but riskier to the issuer because payments are mandatory.

    Stakeholder groups and what they want

    • Shareholders: maximize the long-run value of equity.
    • Debtholders/creditors: timely payment; stable, low-risk cash flows; covenant compliance.
    • Board of directors: elected by shareholders to oversee management on their behalf.
    • Managers and employees: pay, job security, career opportunity — they hold undiversified human capital tied to the firm, so they can be more risk-averse than diversified shareholders want.
    • Customers: product quality, price, safety, ongoing support.
    • Suppliers: getting paid on time; a stable, growing buyer relationship.
    • Governments/regulators: tax revenue, legal compliance; society-wide interests.
    • Communities and the public: jobs, environment, social effects.

    Two frameworks: shareholder theory says the corporation's objective is maximizing shareholder value (within law and ethics); stakeholder theory says management should balance the interests of all stakeholder groups, which is broader but harder to measure and can blur accountability.

    Principal–agent relationships and conflicts

    A principal hires an agent to act on the principal's behalf; conflict arises because the agent has their own interests and better information (information asymmetry). Classic corporate examples:

    • Shareholders vs. managers/directors: managers may consume perks, empire-build, entrench themselves, or take too little risk.
    • Shareholders vs. debtholders: shareholders can transfer value from lenders by taking on riskier assets, issuing more debt, or paying themselves large dividends after borrowing (why covenants exist).
    • Controlling vs. minority shareholders: the controlling group can extract private benefits (detailed in the next section).
    • Customers/suppliers vs. shareholders: e.g., cutting product safety or stretching supplier payments to boost short-term profit.

    ESG considerations

    ESG — environmental, social, and governance factors — captures stakeholder impacts that traditional financial analysis can miss but that affect long-run value: environmental fines and stranded assets, product-safety scandals, labor disputes, weak boards. ESG integration means incorporating material ESG factors into investment analysis and valuation (e.g., higher discount rate or lower cash flows for a firm with big climate-transition exposure), as distinct from values-driven exclusionary screening. Corporate issuers respond through disclosure, board oversight of sustainability, and financing instruments such as green bonds.

    Exam trap. "Debt is lower risk" is true from the investor's perspective. From the issuer's perspective the ranking flips: debt financing is riskier for the company (fixed obligations, default risk) even though it is cheaper. Read carefully whose perspective the question takes.

    Question. After issuing bonds with few covenants, Ristow Corp.'s shareholders vote to sell the firm's stable utility division and reinvest the proceeds in a speculative mining venture, and to pay a large special dividend. Bondholders object. Which conflict is this, and why do the two groups disagree?

    Solution. This is the shareholder–debtholder principal–agent conflict. Shareholders hold a residual, option-like claim: shifting to riskier assets raises their potential upside while limited liability caps their downside, and the special dividend moves cash out of creditors' reach. Bondholders' upside is fixed at the promised coupons and principal, so extra asset risk and cash leakage only increase their probability of loss without any offsetting gain. Value is transferred from bondholders to shareholders. Stronger covenants (limits on asset sales, dividends, and additional debt) are the standard mechanism to prevent exactly this.

    3. Corporate Governance: Conflicts, Mechanisms, and Risks

    Corporate governance is the system of internal controls, procedures, and external pressures that defines rights and responsibilities among managers, the board, shareholders, and other stakeholders — and manages the conflicts of interest between them. Exam questions here are conceptual: identify the conflict, name the mechanism, or spot the governance weakness.

    The two central conflicts

    • Shareholders vs. managers (the classic agency problem). Dispersed shareholders cannot monitor daily decisions; managers may pursue excessive pay and perks, low-value "empire-building" acquisitions (bigger firm → bigger pay and prestige), entrenchment (anti-takeover defenses, stacking the board with allies), excessive caution to protect their jobs, or short-termism to hit bonus targets.
    • Controlling vs. minority shareholders. A shareholder (family, founder, government) with voting control can extract private benefits at minority expense: related-party transactions at off-market prices ("tunneling"), appointing family to key posts, or approving takeovers that favor the controller. Dual-class shares magnify this by separating voting power from economic ownership. Straight (statutory) voting — one share, one vote per board seat — lets a majority holder elect every director; cumulative voting (shareholders can concentrate all their votes on one candidate) helps minorities win board representation.

    Governance mechanisms

    Shareholder mechanisms:

    • Annual and extraordinary general meetings (AGM/EGM) where shareholders vote on directors, auditors, and major transactions — including by proxy (authorizing someone else to vote their shares).
    • Activist investors who accumulate stakes and press for change; proxy fights (soliciting other shareholders' proxies to win board seats); tender offers and the broader market for corporate control — the threat of a hostile takeover disciplines bad managers.
    • Say on pay: a shareholder vote on executive compensation. In most jurisdictions it is advisory (non-binding), but a failed vote is a strong public signal that pressures the board.
    • Lawsuits (derivative actions) and, ultimately, selling the shares.

    Creditor mechanisms: covenants in bond indentures and loan agreements (limits on leverage, dividends, asset sales; required ratios), collateral, and creditor committees in restructuring or bankruptcy.

    Board and management mechanisms:

    • The board of directors is elected by shareholders to direct strategy, hire/fire and pay the CEO, and oversee risk, audit, and compliance. Effective boards have a majority of independent directors (no material ties to the company), relevant and diverse expertise, and are often criticized for CEO duality (CEO also chairing the board). Boards may be staggered (only a fraction of seats up for election each year), which provides continuity but entrenches directors and weakens takeover discipline. One-tier boards mix executive and non-executive directors; some countries (e.g., Germany) use two-tier boards with a separate supervisory board.
    • Key committees, ideally composed of independent directors:
      • Audit committee — oversees financial reporting, internal controls, and the external auditor (independence here is most critical).
      • Compensation (remuneration) committee — sets executive pay and its link to performance.
      • Nomination committee — recruits directors and plans CEO succession.
      • Others as needed: risk committee, investment committee, governance/ESG committee.
    • Compensation design: equity grants, stock options, and long-term incentive plans with vesting and clawback provisions align managers with shareholders — though poorly designed equity pay can encourage short-termism or excessive risk-taking.

    External mechanisms: company law and regulation, stock-exchange listing requirements, mandatory audits and disclosure, credit-rating agencies, analysts, and the media.

    Risks of weak governance — and benefits of strong governance

    • Weak governance risks: poor control environment and accounting fraud, self-dealing and tunneling, value-destroying acquisitions, regulatory penalties, default and bankruptcy, and reputational collapse. These show up for investors as lower valuations, higher borrowing costs, and sudden losses.
    • Strong governance benefits: better operating results and control over risk, lower cost of debt and equity (investors demand less compensation for agency risk), and higher firm value.
    Key point. Match mechanism to conflict. Covenants protect creditors. Independent boards, say on pay, activists, and the takeover market discipline managers. Cumulative voting and rules on related-party transactions protect minority shareholders.
    Exam trap. Say-on-pay votes are usually advisory, not binding. A staggered board is a defense against takeovers and generally weakens shareholder power, even though it is sometimes justified as providing continuity. And "independent director" means independent of management and controlling owners — not merely "non-executive."

    Question. Volta SA's founder owns 18% of the economics but 60% of the votes via super-voting shares. The board: the founder is chair and CEO; the audit committee includes the founder's brother, who is also the company's main parts supplier. Volta buys parts from the brother's firm at prices 20% above market. Identify the governance problems and the investors most at risk.

    Solution. (1) Dual-class structure separates control from economic ownership — a controlling-vs-minority conflict. (2) CEO duality: the founder chairs the board that should supervise him. (3) The audit committee is not independent — a related party (the brother) sits on the committee that oversees reporting and controls. (4) The above-market parts purchases are a related-party transaction that tunnels value out of the company to the founder's family. Minority common shareholders are most at risk: each dollar of overpayment costs them 82% of the economics while the family recaptures it through the supplier. Fixes: independent audit committee, related-party transaction approval by independent directors/minority shareholders, separate chair and CEO.

    4. Working Capital and Liquidity

    Liquidity is the ability to meet short-term obligations as they come due. This module is quantitative: you must compute the operating and cash conversion cycles from turnover data and interpret what makes them better or worse. It links directly to the activity and liquidity ratios in Financial Statement Analysis.

    Working capital and the cash cycle

    Working capital = current assets − current liabilities
    Net working capital (narrow) = (current assets excl. cash and securities) − (current liabilities excl. short-term/current debt)

    A firm buys inventory (often on credit from suppliers), sells it (often on credit to customers), and finally collects cash. Three "days" measures track each leg (365-day convention):

    Days of inventory on hand: DOH = 365 ⁄ inventory turnover = 365 × (avg. inventory ⁄ COGS)
    Days sales outstanding: DSO = 365 ⁄ receivables turnover = 365 × (avg. receivables ⁄ revenue)
    Days payables outstanding: DPO = 365 ⁄ payables turnover = 365 × (avg. payables ⁄ purchases)
    Operating cycle = DOH + DSO
    Cash conversion cycle (net operating cycle): CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO

    The operating cycle is the time from buying inventory to collecting cash from customers. The CCC subtracts the free financing suppliers provide: it is the number of days the firm's own cash is tied up. Shorter is generally better — less financing needed — though a CCC squeezed by aggressively stretching payables can damage supplier relationships, and one shortened by holding too little inventory risks stockouts.

    Question. Marlin Co. reports: revenue 912.5, COGS 730, average inventory 90, average receivables 75, average payables 60 (all $ millions; use purchases ≈ COGS, 365 days). Compute DOH, DSO, DPO, the operating cycle, and the cash conversion cycle.

    Solution. Inventory turnover = 730 ⁄ 90 = 8.111 → DOH = 365 ⁄ 8.111 = 45.0 days.
    Receivables turnover = 912.5 ⁄ 75 = 12.167 → DSO = 365 ⁄ 12.167 = 30.0 days.
    Payables turnover = 730 ⁄ 60 = 12.167 → DPO = 365 ⁄ 12.167 = 30.0 days.
    Operating cycle = 45 + 30 = 75 days. CCC = 45 + 30 − 30 = 45 days: Marlin finances 45 days of its operating cycle with its own capital; suppliers finance the other 30.

    Sources of liquidity

    • Primary sources — ready and routine: cash and marketable securities, cash flow from operations, and existing borrowing capacity such as committed bank lines of credit and trade credit from suppliers. Using them does not disrupt normal operations.
    • Secondary sources — costly signals of stress: renegotiating debt, selling assets, liquidating inventory at a discount, or filing for bankruptcy protection. Using them can change the firm's financial and operating position.

    Drags and pulls on liquidity: a drag delays cash coming in (uncollected/bad receivables, obsolete inventory, tight credit from lenders); a pull accelerates cash going out (suppliers demanding earlier payment, reduced credit limits, covenants forcing paydowns).

    Measuring liquidity

    Current ratio = current assets ⁄ current liabilities
    Quick ratio = (cash + marketable securities + receivables) ⁄ current liabilities
    Cash ratio = (cash + marketable securities) ⁄ current liabilities

    Each successive ratio strips out less-liquid assets (inventory, then receivables). Ratios well below peers — or falling over time — flag rising liquidity risk; extremely high ratios may signal idle, low-return assets.

    Managing the pieces

    • Receivables: set credit terms (e.g., "2/10 net 30" — 2% discount if paid in 10 days, otherwise full amount in 30), monitor an aging schedule, weigh looser terms (more sales, more bad debts and higher DSO) against tighter ones.
    • Inventory: balance carrying costs (storage, insurance, obsolescence, financing) against stockout costs; approaches range from just-in-time to safety stock.
    • Payables: pay on the due date, not early — but take discounts when they are worth more than the cost of funds. Forgoing "2/10 net 30" means paying 2% to keep the money 20 extra days, an annualized cost of (1 + 2⁄98)365/20 − 1 ≈ 44.6% — almost always worth borrowing to take the discount.

    Short-term financing

    • Uncommitted lines of credit — bank may refuse to lend; cheapest, least reliable.
    • Committed lines / revolving credit agreements — bank is contractually obligated (fee charged on the unused portion); revolvers are the strongest, often multi-year form.
    • Secured ("asset-based") loans — borrowing against receivables or inventory; factoring is selling receivables outright at a discount.
    • Commercial paper — short-term unsecured notes issued by large, high-credit-quality firms; usually cheaper than bank borrowing but only available to strong issuers (typically backstopped by a bank line).
    Key point. CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO. To shorten it: sell inventory faster (↓DOH), collect faster (↓DSO), pay suppliers slower (↑DPO). Only DPO enters with a minus sign.
    Exam trap. Sign error on DPO is the classic mistake — a firm with a long DPO has a shorter CCC. Also note some firms (large retailers) have a negative CCC: they collect cash from customers before paying suppliers. That is a strength, not distress.

    5. Capital Investments and Capital Allocation

    Capital allocation is how a firm evaluates and ranks long-term investments. NPV, IRR, and payback calculations are near-guaranteed exam material, along with knowing when NPV and IRR disagree and why NPV wins.

    Types of capital projects

    • Maintenance / going-concern — replace worn equipment, continue current operations; low risk, routine analysis.
    • Regulatory / compliance — required by law or safety rules; may have negative NPV on their own but must be done (or the business exited).
    • Expansion — grow existing business; more uncertainty, fuller analysis.
    • New lines of business / other — new products, M&A; highest uncertainty, most complex analysis (often involving real options).

    Principles: decisions are based on incremental after-tax cash flows (not accounting income), include opportunity costs (what a resource could earn in its next-best use) and externalities like cannibalization of existing sales, exclude sunk costs and financing costs (financing is captured in the discount rate), and reflect the timing of cash flows.

    Net present value (NPV)

    NPV = Σt=0T CFt ⁄ (1 + r)t   (CF0 is usually negative — the outlay)

    r is the required rate of return (opportunity cost of capital) for the project's risk. Rule: accept if NPV > 0. NPV is the value added to the firm in today's dollars — accepting a positive-NPV project should raise shareholder wealth by the NPV amount.

    Internal rate of return (IRR)

    IRR is the discount rate that makes NPV = 0:   Σ CFt ⁄ (1 + IRR)t = 0

    Rule: accept if IRR > r (the required return, the "hurdle rate"). IRR is intuitive — a percentage return — but it implicitly assumes interim cash flows are reinvested at the IRR, which is unrealistic for high-IRR projects.

    Payback measures (know the flaws)

    • Payback period — years to recover the initial outlay from cumulative cash flows. Simple liquidity indicator, but ignores time value of money and all cash flows after payback; not a measure of profitability.
    • Discounted payback — same idea using discounted cash flows; fixes time value but still ignores post-payback cash flows.

    Return on invested capital (ROIC)

    ROIC = after-tax operating profit ⁄ average invested capital  (invested capital = total debt + equity capital)

    ROIC measures how effectively the whole company converts invested capital into after-tax operating profit. A firm creates value when ROIC > cost of capital. Unlike NPV/IRR (forward-looking, project-level), ROIC is computed from financial statements for the company as a whole.

    Question. A project costs $100,000 today and returns $40,000 at the end of each year for 4 years. The required return is 10%. Compute NPV, IRR, payback period, and discounted payback. Accept or reject?

    Solution. NPV: PV of the 4-year, $40,000 annuity at 10% = 40,000 × [1 − 1.10−4] ⁄ 0.10 = 40,000 × 3.1699 = 126,795. NPV = 126,795 − 100,000 = +$26,795. BA II Plus: CF0=−100000, C01=40000, F01=4; NPV, I=10, CPT → 26,794.62.
    IRR: same CF worksheet → IRR CPT = 21.86%, well above the 10% hurdle.
    Payback: 100,000 ⁄ 40,000 = 2.5 years.
    Discounted payback: discounted CFs are 36,364; 33,058; 30,053; 27,321. Cumulative after year 3 = 99,475, still $525 short; 525 ⁄ 27,321 = 0.02 → ≈ 3.02 years.
    Both NPV > 0 and IRR > 10%: accept.

    When NPV and IRR conflict — and multiple IRRs

    • For a single conventional project (one outflow, then inflows), NPV and IRR always agree on accept/reject.
    • For mutually exclusive projects they can rank differently, because of differences in project size/scale or in cash-flow timing (early-cash projects look better under IRR's aggressive reinvestment assumption). When they conflict, choose the higher NPV — NPV measures dollars of wealth created and assumes reinvestment at the realistic required return.
    • Unconventional cash flows (signs change more than once — e.g., outflow, inflows, then a big cleanup cost) can produce multiple IRRs (up to one per sign change) or no IRR at all. NPV always exists and stays reliable.

    Question. Mutually exclusive projects, r = 10%. Project A: CF0 = −1,000, year 1 = +1,300 (IRR = 30%). Project B: CF0 = −10,000, year 1 = +12,000 (IRR = 20%). Which should the firm take?

    Solution. NPV(A) = −1,000 + 1,300 ⁄ 1.10 = −1,000 + 1,181.82 = +181.82. NPV(B) = −10,000 + 12,000 ⁄ 1.10 = −10,000 + 10,909.09 = +909.09. IRR ranks A first (30% > 20%), but B adds five times more wealth. Because the projects are mutually exclusive and differ in scale, follow NPV: choose B. IRR misleads because a huge percentage return on a small base can create fewer dollars than a modest return on a large base.

    Capital allocation pitfalls

    • Counting sunk costs (already spent, unrecoverable — irrelevant) or ignoring opportunity costs and cannibalization.
    • Inertia: this year's budget anchored on last year's rather than zero-based analysis.
    • Failing to consider alternatives or generate ideas; pushing pet projects.
    • Basing decisions on accounting metrics (EPS, ROE) instead of incremental cash flows — managers paid on EPS may reject positive-NPV projects that dilute near-term EPS.
    • Wrong discount rate (company WACC applied to a project of different risk), overly optimistic forecasts, and ignoring internal capital-rationing dynamics.

    Real options

    Real options are rights (not obligations) embedded in projects to alter decisions later, which add value on top of static NPV:

    • Timing option — delay investing until more information arrives.
    • Sizing options — abandonment (exit and recover salvage value if things go badly) and growth/expansion (scale up if things go well).
    • Flexibility options — change prices or the production process (e.g., switch fuel or inputs).
    • Fundamental options — the whole project's payoff depends on an underlying price (e.g., a mine that operates only when the metal price is high).
    Project value = NPV (without options) − option cost + option value

    A project with negative static NPV can still be worth accepting if its real options are valuable enough.

    Key point. NPV is the gold standard: it uses all cash flows, time value, and a realistic reinvestment assumption, and it measures wealth created in dollars. Every other criterion is judged by how it deviates from NPV.
    Exam trap. "The firm already spent $2 million on a feasibility study" — sunk, exclude it. IRR's reinvestment assumption (at the IRR) vs. NPV's (at the required return) is a favorite conceptual question, as is the number of possible IRRs when cash-flow signs change twice (answer: up to two, possibly none).

    6. Capital Structure

    Capital structure is the mix of debt and equity financing. You need the Modigliani–Miller (MM) logic at an intuition level, the trade-off and pecking-order theories, a solid WACC calculation, and the leverage measures (DOL, DFL, breakeven).

    Weighted-average cost of capital (WACC)

    WACC = wd rd(1 − t) + wp rp + we re

    Weights should be target capital-structure weights at market values (not book values). Only debt gets the (1 − t) adjustment, because interest — unlike dividends — is tax-deductible: the government effectively pays t of the interest bill (the interest tax shield).

    Question. Kestrel Inc. targets 30% debt, 10% preferred, 60% common equity (market values). Pre-tax cost of debt 6%, cost of preferred 7%, cost of equity 11%, marginal tax rate 25%. Its WACC is closest to?

    Solution. After-tax cost of debt = 6% × (1 − 0.25) = 4.5%.
    WACC = 0.30 × 4.5% + 0.10 × 7% + 0.60 × 11% = 1.35% + 0.70% + 6.60% = 8.65%.
    Common error: forgetting the tax adjustment gives 0.30 × 6% + 0.70% + 6.60% = 9.10% — a typical distractor. Note preferred dividends are not tax-deductible, so rp gets no (1 − t).

    Modigliani–Miller: the benchmark logic

    MM asked: in a perfect market (no taxes, no bankruptcy costs, no information asymmetry, equal borrowing rates), does the debt–equity mix matter? Their answer frames everything else:

    • MM Proposition I (no taxes): capital structure is irrelevant — VL = VU. Firm value depends on the assets' cash flows, not how the pie is sliced between debt and equity holders. (If it did matter, investors could replicate or undo leverage themselves — "homemade leverage" — and arbitrage away any difference.)
    • MM Proposition II (no taxes): the cost of equity rises linearly with leverage — re = r0 + (r0 − rd) × D⁄E — exactly offsetting the substitution of cheap debt for expensive equity, so WACC stays constant. Cheap debt is not a free lunch: adding it makes the remaining equity riskier.
    • With corporate taxes: interest deductibility creates the tax shield, so VL = VU + t × D. Cost of equity still rises — re = r0 + (r0 − rd)(1 − t) × D⁄E — but more slowly, so WACC falls as debt rises. Taken literally, the optimum would be ~100% debt, which is obviously wrong in practice — because MM-with-taxes ignores financial distress.

    Costs of financial distress and the static trade-off theory

    Costs of financial distress grow with leverage: direct costs (legal and administrative costs of bankruptcy/reorganization) and larger indirect costs (lost customers and suppliers, departing employees, forgone investment, fire-sale asset prices). Expected distress cost = probability of distress (rises with leverage and with operating risk) × magnitude (worse for firms with few tangible, salable assets).

    Static trade-off: VL = VU + t×D − PV(expected costs of financial distress)

    Firm value first rises with debt (tax shield dominates), then falls (distress costs dominate). The optimal capital structure is the debt level where the marginal tax benefit equals the marginal distress cost — equivalently, where WACC is minimized and firm value is maximized. Firms with stable cash flows and tangible assets (utilities) can carry more debt than volatile, intangible-asset firms (software, biotech).

    Pecking order theory and other practical considerations

    • Pecking order (Myers): because of information asymmetry, managers prefer financing that reveals the least: internal funds first, then debt, then new equity last. Issuing equity signals managers may think shares are overvalued, so announcements of equity issues tend to knock the price down. Implication: capital structure is partly a by-product of past profitability, not a target — highly profitable firms may carry little debt simply because they never needed external funds.
    • In practice, target structures move with the life cycle: start-ups are nearly all equity (no stable cash flows to support debt), growth firms add some debt, and mature firms with dependable cash flows carry the most (cheapest) debt. Agency costs of equity (Jensen's free-cash-flow argument) suggest debt can discipline managers by committing cash to interest payments.

    Leverage: DOL, DFL, DTL, and breakeven

    Leverage is the use of fixed costs — fixed operating costs (rent, depreciation, salaried staff) and fixed financing costs (interest). Fixed costs magnify how a change in sales translates into changes in profit, in both directions.

    DOL = %ΔEBIT ⁄ %Δsales = Q(P − V) ⁄ [Q(P − V) − F]
    DFL = %ΔEPS ⁄ %ΔEBIT = EBIT ⁄ (EBIT − I)
    DTL = DOL × DFL = %ΔEPS ⁄ %Δsales
    Operating breakeven: QOBE = F ⁄ (P − V)   •   Total breakeven: QBE = (F + I) ⁄ (P − V)

    where Q = units, P = price, V = variable cost per unit, F = fixed operating costs, I = fixed financing costs. Note DOL and DFL are computed at a given level of sales — they change as Q changes, exploding near breakeven.

    Question. Osprey Ltd. sells units at P = $50 with variable cost V = $30, fixed operating costs F = $100,000, and interest I = $40,000. At Q = 10,000 units: compute DOL, DFL, DTL, and both breakeven quantities. If sales rise 10%, what happens to EPS?

    Solution. Contribution per unit = 50 − 30 = $20. EBIT = 10,000 × 20 − 100,000 = $100,000.
    DOL = (10,000 × 20) ⁄ (200,000 − 100,000) = 200,000 ⁄ 100,000 = 2.0.
    DFL = 100,000 ⁄ (100,000 − 40,000) = 1.667.
    DTL = 2.0 × 1.667 = 3.33 → a 10% sales rise lifts EPS by ≈ 33.3% (and a 10% fall cuts it 33.3% — leverage cuts both ways).
    Operating breakeven = 100,000 ⁄ 20 = 5,000 units. Total breakeven = 140,000 ⁄ 20 = 7,000 units.

    Key point. MM no-tax: structure irrelevant, WACC flat. MM with tax: VL = VU + tD, WACC falls with debt. Trade-off theory adds distress costs and produces an interior optimum where WACC is minimized. Pecking order: internal → debt → equity.
    Exam trap. Apply (1 − t) to debt only — never to preferred or equity. Use target/market-value weights, not book values. And under MM Proposition II (no taxes), issuing "cheap" debt does NOT lower WACC — the rising cost of equity exactly offsets it.

    7. Business Models

    This module is conceptual: given a description of a company, identify its business model features, pricing approach, and channel strategy. It supplies vocabulary used later in Equity industry analysis.

    What a business model describes

    A business model explains how a firm creates value and gets paid for it. A complete description answers:

    • Who are the customers (which segments)?
    • What does the firm offer — the product/service and its value proposition: why customers choose this offering at this price over alternatives (some mix of price, quality, convenience, brand, service)?
    • Where / how delivered — the channel strategy: direct sales vs. intermediaries, physical vs. digital, omnichannel (integrated physical + digital); B2B (business-to-business) vs. B2C (business-to-consumer); drop-shipping (seller never holds inventory).
    • How much — the pricing and revenue model.
    • How the firm makes it work profitably — key assets, suppliers, and the cost structure that supports the price (the profitability logic connects the business model to margins and returns you analyze in FSA).

    Pricing and revenue models

    • Value-based pricing — set by the value delivered to the customer; cost-based pricing — cost plus a markup.
    • Price discrimination — different prices to different users for the same product: tiered pricing (volume tiers), dynamic pricing (peak/off-peak, surge), auction pricing.
    • For rapid growth: penetration pricing (price low, even at a loss, to build share — streaming services), freemium (basic version free, pay to upgrade), hidden revenue (users pay nothing; advertisers pay — search engines, social media).
    • Alternatives to outright sale: subscription/recurring, licensing, franchising, leasing/rental.
    • Bundling complementary products; razor-and-blades (cheap device, high-margin consumables); add-on pricing (low base price, priced options).

    Network effects and platform businesses

    A network effect means the product becomes more valuable to each user as more users join (payment networks, social media, marketplaces). Platform businesses monetize networks of users rather than selling a product they make; many exhibit two-sided network effects — more riders attract more drivers, which attracts more riders. Consequences: winner-take-most competitive dynamics, and value that scales with little incremental capital (contrast with a linear manufacturer, which must add plant to add output). A crowdsourcing model has users contribute the content or work directly (reviews, open-source software).

    Other model features worth naming

    • Value chain (the activities the firm itself performs that add value) vs. the broader supply chain (all steps from raw material to end customer, whoever performs them).
    • Asset-light models: shift capital-intensive assets to counterparties (franchising, drop-shipping).
    • Direct-to-customer disintermediation: cutting out wholesalers/retailers via e-commerce.
    Key point. Be able to label a described firm quickly: who pays (B2B/B2C/advertisers), the pricing model (freemium, subscription, razor-and-blades...), the channel (direct, omnichannel, platform), and whether network effects make scale self-reinforcing.
    Exam trap. "Hidden revenue" does not mean fraud — it means end users are not the payers (advertisers are). And economies of scale (unit costs fall as output rises) are not the same thing as network effects (product value to users rises as users join); a firm can have either without the other.

    Question. RideLink operates an app matching independent drivers with passengers; it owns no vehicles, takes a percentage of each fare, raises prices automatically during peak demand, and grows because more drivers mean shorter waits, which attracts more passengers. Classify its business model features.

    Solution. RideLink is a platform business (it monetizes a network, not a product it manufactures) with two-sided network effects (drivers ↔ passengers reinforcing each other). It is asset-light — capital-intensive assets (cars) belong to drivers — and largely B2C on the passenger side. Revenue model: a commission/take-rate on each transaction, with dynamic pricing (surge pricing is peak-load price discrimination). Its value proposition to passengers is convenience and short wait times; to drivers, access to demand without marketing costs.
